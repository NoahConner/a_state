Texas Car Insurance Laws: Understanding the 30/60/25 Rule

In Texas, car insurance laws require drivers to have basic coverage in case of an accident. These rules apply to anyone purchasing vehicle insurance in Texas to legally operate a car in the state. One of the key requirements is the 30/60/25 rule, shorthand for the minimum liability coverage you must carry as required by the Texas Department of Insurance.

The Breakdown of 30/60/25

$30,000 (Bodily Injury per Person)

This is the maximum amount your insurance will pay for medical bills, lost wages, or funeral expenses if someone is injured in an accident you cause. It’s important to note that if multiple people are injured, this limit applies to each person separately, meaning if two people are hurt, the $30,000 limit would be split between them.

$60,000 (Bodily Injury per Accident)

This is the total amount available to all people injured in a single accident. So, if three people are injured in an accident you cause, the $60,000 will be divided among them. While this sounds like a lot, depending on the severity of the injuries, this amount can be used up quickly in medical bills.

$25,000 (Property Damage)

This covers damage you cause to someone else’s vehicle or property, such as fences or utility poles. If the other person’s car is damaged, your insurance will pay for repairs or replacement, up to $25,000. However, if the damage exceeds this amount, you could be personally liable for the difference.

Texas Auto Insurance Minimum Coverage Requirements

Minimum coverage refers to the basic liability insurance that all drivers are required to have. While this satisfies legal requirements, many drivers explore affordable auto insurance in Texas options that offer additional protection beyond the minimum. This coverage helps pay for injuries or damages you cause to others, but it doesn't cover your vehicle or any injuries you sustain.

Is "Full Coverage" Required in the Lone Star State?

When it comes to auto insurance in Texas, there's a common misconception that "Full Coverage" is mandatory. The truth is, Texas law only requires liability insurance, specifically the 30/60/25 coverage, which covers damage you cause to others, not your own vehicle.  While liability meets legal requirements, many drivers choose full coverage policies because they offer stronger protection and are often recommended when searching for the best auto insurance.

What "Full Coverage" Actually Means

Full Coverage is a term often used to describe a combination of coverages rather than a single policy. It usually includes:

Liability Insurance

The basic coverage required by law in Texas (30/60/25).

Collision Coverage

Pays for repairs to your car after a crash, no matter who’s at fault. If you’re in an accident and it’s your fault, this helps fix your car.

Comprehensive Coverage

Covers non-collision incidents like hail damage, flooding, or theft, which are pretty common in Texas

Standard and Optional Auto Insurance Coverages in Texas

When it comes to auto insurance, there are two main types of coverage to consider: standard and optional. Many drivers compare policies to find the insurance that balances protection and cost. Standard coverages protect you from the most common risks on the road, while optional add-ons allow drivers to customize their vehicle insurance in Texas based on their needs.

Standard Coverages (Required or Commonly Included)
Bodily Injury Liability

This coverage helps pay for medical bills, lost wages, and legal fees if you're responsible for an accident that injures others. In Texas, it's required by law to have at least $30,000 per person and $60,000 per accident in bodily injury liability.

Personal Injury Protection (PIP)

This insurance plan covers your medical bills and lost wages if you're injured in an accident, regardless of fault. It's especially useful if you don't have health insurance or if your medical costs exceed your regular coverage limits.

Property Damage Liability

Property damage liability covers the cost of repairing or replacing another person's vehicle or property that you damage in an accident. The minimum required coverage in Texas is $25,000, but you may want higher limits to better protect yourself.

Uninsured / Underinsured Motorist Coverage (UM/UIM)

This coverage protects you if you're in an accident with someone who either doesn't have insurance or doesn't have enough to cover the costs. It helps pay for your medical bills and damages when the at-fault driver is underinsured or uninsured.

Optional Coverages (Add-Ons)
Comprehensive Coverage

This part protects against damage that isn't caused by a collision, such as theft, hail damage, or vandalism. It's especially important in Texas, where severe weather, such as hailstorms and floods, is common.

Medical Payments (MedPay)

MedPay covers medical bills for you and your passengers after an accident, regardless of who's at fault. It's a comprehensive and helpful option if you want to ensure that medical expenses are covered in addition to your PIP or health insurance.

Gap Insurance

Gap insurance is useful if you owe more on your car loan or lease than the car is worth. If your car is totaled in an accident, gap insurance helps cover the difference between what you owe and what your insurance pays.

How Much Does Car Insurance Cost in Texas?

Car insurance rates in Texas can vary widely depending on several factors. On average, drivers in the state can expect to pay around $85 per month for the minimum liability coverage and about $215 per month for full coverage. However, your actual cost can vary based on where you live, your driving history, and other factors.

Texas car insurance rates are generally higher than in some other states due to factors such as traffic congestion, frequent accidents, and the state's vulnerability to severe weather. Compared with other states, Texas tends to have higher insurance costs, especially in urban areas. States with lower premiums typically have fewer traffic issues, fewer accidents, and fewer weather-related claims, all of which contribute to lower rates.

Independent Insurance Agency vs. Captive Agent: Why Choice Saves You Money

When shopping for auto insurance in Texas, the type of agent you work with can make a significant difference in both price and coverage. Many drivers assume all insurance agents work the same way, but that is not true. There are two main types of agents: captive agents and independent insurance brokers.

Insurance rates change frequently. In 2026, many carriers adjusted pricing due to inflation, repair costs, and increased claims. When you work with a captive agent, you are stuck if that company raises its rates. With an independent broker, your policy can be re-shopped across multiple carriers when prices change. This allows you to maintain affordable auto insurance in Texas without having to search for a new agent every year.

Understanding the difference can help you find cheap auto insurance without sacrificing coverage or service.
